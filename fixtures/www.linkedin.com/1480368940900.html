Using Dogs to Overcome Shyness

Oct 28, 2016
  • 1,748 views
  • 575 Likes
  • 38 Comments
  • Share on LinkedIn
  • Share on Facebook
  • Share on Twitter

With rare exceptions, I am not the life of the party. I lean towards shyness and have trouble walking up to complete strangers at, say, a party or conference.

Unless they have a dog.

If you own a dog, I'm going to say hello to you.

Actually, that's not exactly true. I'm going to ask you something about your dog, such as "is it okay to pat him?" or "what kind of dog is that?". In doing so, the barrier between us will disappear, and we can start talking.

Your dog will tell me more about you than I could ever learn from watching you chat with three other strangers while I hug the corner of a room at a party. In many ways, dogs reflect their owners. If you're calm, your dog will learn to be calm. Every now and then I see a nervous dog with ears pulled back and tail between its legs, and her owner is pretty much in a similar position.

I leave them alone.

But most importantly, dogs provide me with a "no-risk" way to approach you. If you ask me not to pat your dog, it could be a reflection of your dog's nervousness or your own tight schedule. It doesn't come across as a personal rejection of me.

Likewise, if I say hi to your dog and you basically ignore me, I can easily slip away after a moment, no big deal.

Take away the dog, and these social interactions are much riskier.

I was once with a group of friends waiting for a chairlift ride on a gorgeous autumn morning. A family had two Bernese Mountain dogs, and I greeted one of them, then sat down on the ground next to him. Five seconds later, this enormous dog sat in my lap, but his sister was nervous and wouldn't come close. The lift was slow to open, so I sat there for ten minutes until his sister decided I now smelled like her brother and came over.

In the process, I demonstrated:

  • Patience
  • Compassion
  • Friendliness

Guess what? The other family reciprocated, and we had a lovely chat.

Your might think that the moral of this story is: Bruce likes dogs. That's certainly true, but it's not the moral.

When you host a party, meeting, or social gathering, your job is to play the role I just described with regards to dogs. You need to make people comfortable together, and to provide no-risk ways for them to meet and interact. This takes warmth, creativity, and effort.

If you like, you could try inviting ten puppies to join you. That would do the trick. But more likely it will be up to you to search out people like me, over there in the corner, and pull them into the mainstream.
